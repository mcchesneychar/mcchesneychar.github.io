Nearest Neighbors

Distance-based prediction with k and a metric of your choice

How it works

This was the third-section assignment for my Predictive Analytics (ECON 475) class — a from-scratch implementation of k-nearest neighbors. To predict a new observation, the algorithm computes its distance to every training point, takes the closest k, and returns the majority label among them.

The interesting design choice is the distance metric. Euclidean measures straight-line distance and is the default; Manhattan sums the absolute differences (better when features move on independent grids); Minkowski-3 generalizes both with a cubic exponent; cosine measures the angle between vectors and is magnitude-invariant — critical when class identity lives in direction rather than scale.

The same dataset is then handed to sklearn's KNeighborsClassifier with two backends — kd_tree and brute — to compare wall-clock time.

Use cases

k-NN is the simplest possible "intelligent" classifier — there's no training step at all, just a stored dataset and a distance function. That makes it a great baseline for tabular problems and a natural choice for recommender systems, anomaly detection (flag points whose nearest neighbors are far away), and image / document retrieval where similarity in some learned embedding space is the prediction.

Its main weaknesses are computational (every prediction touches every training point unless you use a spatial index like a kd-tree) and statistical: high-dimensional spaces dilute distance, so feature scaling and dimensionality reduction matter a lot.
